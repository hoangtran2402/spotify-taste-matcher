Exploring the Dataset

The five visualizations below reveal patterns in 2,000 Spotify tracks spanning 1998–2020. Use the interactive controls to dig deeper — hover over data points for details, click genres to isolate them, or drag the year slider to watch the top-artists chart update in real time.

1 · Distribution of Popularity by Genre

D3.js box plot (Altair-equivalent: mark_boxplot) · Interaction: click genre box to isolate

Takeaway: Latin and rock lead in median popularity (both ~70), followed closely by hip-hop and R&B (~65–68). Pop sits mid-pack despite being the most represented genre in the dataset, and it has the widest spread — outliers extend all the way down to near 0, reflecting the many obscure tracks in the catalog alongside mainstream hits. Dance/Electronic and country have the lowest medians (~55), suggesting they are underrepresented in the chart-hit tier of this dataset. Click any box to isolate that genre; click again to reset.

2 · Energy vs. Danceability

D3.js · Interaction: hover tooltip + genre filter buttons

Takeaway: Energy and danceability are mildly positively correlated, but the relationship is loose — many high-energy rock tracks score low on danceability, while hip-hop clusters in the upper-right (high on both). The genre filter buttons let you isolate one genre at a time to compare distributions directly. Circle size encodes popularity: larger circles are more popular songs.

3 · Feature Correlation Heatmap

D3.js · Interaction: hover to see exact correlation value

Takeaway: Energy and loudness share the strongest positive correlation (r = 0.65), confirming that louder mixes are engineered for intensity. Acousticness is negatively correlated with both energy (r = −0.45) and loudness (r = −0.31) — acoustic recordings are inherently quieter and softer. Valence and danceability are moderately correlated (r = 0.40), meaning happier-sounding songs tend to be more danceable. Notably, popularity is nearly uncorrelated with every other feature (all r values within ±0.05), suggesting chart success in this dataset is not strongly predicted by any single audio attribute alone.

4 · Top 10 Artists by Track Count per Year

D3.js · Interaction: year slider animates bar chart

Takeaway: Dataset coverage is uneven across years. Early 2000s charts are dominated by a handful of pop mega-acts (Destiny's Child, Britney Spears, Eminem). By the 2010s the roster diversifies — Drake and Taylor Swift appear repeatedly but never as overwhelmingly as early pop monopolies. 5 · Average Track Energy Over Time (1998–2020)

Altair / Vega-Lite · Interaction: hover nearest point for exact value

Takeaway: Average energy starts very low in 1998 (~0.45) — likely because the dataset has few tracks from that year — then jumps sharply to ~0.74 by 1999 and stays elevated through the 2000s. It peaks around 2012–2013 at ~0.78, consistent with the loudness-war era and EDM's mainstream breakthrough. After that, energy dips gradually, reaching a low of ~0.65 around 2018, before a sharp spike back to ~0.79 in 2020, possibly driven by the resurgence of high-production pop and hyperpop. Hover over any point to see the exact mean value.

Summary of Findings

Pop and hip-hop together account for over 85% of the dataset, yet latin and rock tracks actually score higher median popularity — suggesting those genres punch above their weight in chart performance despite smaller representation. The moderate energy–loudness correlation (r = 0.65) confirms that users rating "energy" highly will tend to receive loud, produced tracks, while acousticness acts as a near-inverse proxy (r = −0.45 with energy). Crucially, popularity is almost entirely uncorrelated with every audio feature, meaning the recommender's feature-matching approach cannot reliably reproduce what makes a track a hit — only what makes it sound similar. The sharp 2020 energy spike is an outlier worth investigating: if the dataset oversamples a specific high-energy subgenre for that year it could bias recommendations for users who select recent tracks. Future work could normalise within yearly cohorts, add a decade filter to the taste profile, and weight genres by representation to reduce the pop/hip-hop dominance.
